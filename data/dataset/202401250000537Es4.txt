## 1. Introduction

Hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) in water splitting is a highly promising means of obtaining pure hydrogen, which can be powered by the green electricity such as renewable and intermittent wind and solar energy, and high activity electrocatalysts as well, demonstrating huge potential in future clean energy infrastructure. [1] However, HER electrocatalysts applied for industrial water splitting have to sustain harsh conditions, such as large enough current density, ultra-long working period, elevated ambient pressure, and temperature, [2] which bring about great challenges to the durability of electrodes. The performance degradation of the catalyst at industrial current density is mainly caused by the decrease of active site density and the deactivation of active sites. During HER, the catalysts will inevitably loss their active sites due to the site falling off from the catalyst surface due

to the bubbling effect of hydrogen generated on the catalyst surface, especially at elevated current densities. Meanwhile, the active site can be easily deactivated due to factors such as site coverage, Ostwald Ripening, and local corrosion. [3]

Recently, most studies on enhancing the stability of HER catalysts have focused on weakening or preventing the adhesion of bubbles into the catalyst surface and improving the mechanical stability of the catalyst. Liu et al. developed a mechanically stable CuMo$_{6}$S$_{8}$/Cu electrode, [4] successfully achieving stable hydrogen evolution at 2.5 A cm - 2 for over 100 h in alkaline electrolyte. It is also reported that the deactivation of active sites can be prevented by using strong metal support interactions and even metal alloying strategies in HER. Wang et al. successfully loaded Rh single atom onto the nanometer TiC carrier, [5] which can stably evolve hydrogen for 72 h at 500 mA cm - 2 in 1 m KOH. Unfortunately, improved stabilities of the catalysts to certain degrees by the above strategies are usually accompanied by the decreases in HER activity, especially the HER current density, and additionally, the precious metals and the complicated catalyst preparations are involved wherein. Molybdenum oxides (MoO$_{x}$) have been reported to easily dissolve into MoO$_{4}$ 2 - in aqueous solutions at reduced potentials, [6] which can also reversibly decompose into MoO$_{x}$, [7] offering the possibility of exposing active sites. On the other hand, nickel phosphide is recognized as one of the most promising nonprecious metal catalysts toward alkaline HER according to the appropriate hydrogen Gibbs free energy. [8] Based on the above knowledge, we herein propose a novel in situ refreshing strategy to guarantee high HER performance, especially the excellent durability of catalysts at elevated current density, in which multilevel active sites built inside the catalyst could be gradually and continuously exposed during HER along with the falling-off of degraded ones by generated hydrogen bubbles. This in situ refreshing strategy ensures the activity and density of active sites of the electrocatalyst, thus leading to the ultra-stable catalytic activity toward HER at enhanced current densities.

Specifically, we first fabricated a HER electrocatalytic precatalyst, a composite of the nickel phosphate embedded in an amorphous molybdenum oxide matrix named as NiMoOP( Scheme 1-I ), which was activated under a certain reduction potential to dissolve the amorphous molybdenum compound matrix and expose the active sites of nickel species embedded in the interior of the catalyst, obtaining a robust HER catalyst (A-NiMoO-P) (Scheme 1-II). The obtained A-NiMoO-P catalyst displays significantly enhanced HER performance than NiMoO-P owing to the continued exposures of Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ and Ni$_{2}$P active sites resulting from the in situ conversion of partial Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ during the activation, which is ensured by the first amorphous matrix dissolution into the electrolyte and the following dissolution-deposition dynamic equilibrium of the amorphous matrix (Scheme 1-III). Notably, the performance degradation of the constructed A-NiMoO-P catalyst resulting from the coverage and deactivation of active sites at enhanced current density can be effectively circumvented, leading to greatly elevated stability by the continuous active site exposure. As a result, the constructed A-NiMoO-P shows excellent HER performance featuring an ultra-low overpotential of 340 mV to reach 1500 mA cm - 2 and outstanding durability for at least 1000 h at an industrial-applicable current density of â‰ˆ 900 mA cm - $^{2}$.The result of quasi in situ Raman spectroscopy confirms the changes of MoO$_{x}$ phase structure on the catalyst surface and the appearance of Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$, consolidating the surface reconstruction of the

catalyst leading to the excellent stability of A-NiMoO-P at the current density. The HER performance of formed active sites is also verified by density functional theory (DFT) calculation. This work provides a novel strategy for constructing HER catalysts featuring both high performance and extra-high durability at elevated current densities by refreshing the active sites during electrochemical reactions, which is believed to be of great prospective in the practical hydrogen production industry.

## 2. Results and Discussion

The pre-catalyst NiMoO-P was synthesized by hydrothermal treatment and subsequent thermal phosphorylation ( Figure 1 a). Nickel foam (NF) was selected as the substrate owing to its high porosity and conductivity. Briefly, Ni(NO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ and (NH$_{4}$)$_{6}$Mo$_{7}$O$_{24}$ were used as raw chemicals to in situ grow NiMoO$_{4}$ nanorods on NF. [9] Then, NiMoO-P pre-catalyst was prepared by phosphating the NiMoO$_{4}$ precursor via low-temperature treatment, leading to a composite composed of Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ with radial multi-level distribution and amorphous molybdenum oxide matrix. During the electrochemical activation under alkaline HER conditions, molybdenum oxide gradually dissolved into the electrolyte to expose Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ components. Meanwhile, a fraction of Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ in situ converted into Ni$_{2}$P during HER (9 e - + 2 Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ â†’ Ni$_{2}$P + 3PO 4 3 $^{-}$). Then, the A-NiMoO-P catalyst was formed accompanying the in situ electrochemical reconstruction of NiMoO-P.

## 3. Conclusion

This work proposes a novel active site in situ refreshing strategy of electro-catalytic materials to greatly enhance the durability of electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution by water splitting. Briefly, a composite catalyst of Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ being embedded in an amorphous molybdenum compound matrix was fabricated, which will expose the inside Ni(PO$_{3}$)$_{2}$ andinsituformedNi$_{2}$P active sites by the matrix dissolution into the electrolyte without the losses of these active sites. Importantly, the following dissolutiondeposition dynamic equilibrium of the amorphous matrix during the electrochemical HER ensures the effective and long-lasting activation of the catalyst without noticeable activity degradation. The continuous active site refreshing endows the composite catalyst with excellent HER performance featuring an ultra-high current density of 1500 mA cm - 2 at an overpotential of 340 mV, and an extra-long durability for at least 1000 h at an industrialapplicable current density of â‰ˆ 900 mA cm - $^{2}$. This study offers a novel strategy for active site refreshing to create highly active and extra-long lasting HER catalysts for industrial-applicable hydrogen production.
